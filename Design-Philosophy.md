# Design Philosophy

## Brutal, but legible

Cinder Veins is meant to be hard — hard from level 1, and it doesn't let up. That's the identity. But difficulty and confusion are **not** the same thing, and Cinder Veins draws a hard line between them:

- **Difficulty is good.** Enemies hit hard. You have to play carefully, build deliberately, and respect what's on screen.
- **Illegibility is the enemy.** A player should never die to a rule they couldn't see, didn't understand, or were never taught.

Every death should leave the player thinking *"I should have done X,"* not *"...what just happened?"*

## The ESR lesson

Eastern Sun Resurrected proved you can build enormous depth into a D2R mod. But it also overwhelms a lot of new players — and the reason is worth being precise about:

> ESR doesn't overwhelm because it's **deep**. It overwhelms because it's **illegible** — many systems aren't taught in-game, so the wiki becomes *mandatory just to play*.

That's a design failure, not a depth failure. Cinder Veins keeps the depth and fixes the legibility:

- **The game teaches the basics.** Core mechanics should be learnable by playing, not by reading.
- **The wiki holds the deep dives.** Theorycrafters go to the wiki for the deep math and edge cases — but you should be able to *survive and progress* without it.
- **Complexity is layered, not dumped.** Systems unlock and reveal themselves over time, rather than hitting the player all at once.

## Practical rules this produces

1. **Difficulty comes from monster power, not hidden rules.** Crank stats, not obscurity. (This is also why immunities were removed — see [Core Systems](Core-Systems.md).)
2. **Document each custom system the moment it's built.** The wiki grows with the mod, not back-filled at the end (which never happens).
3. **Telegraph the brutal stuff.** If something can one-shot the player, the player should have had a readable signal.
